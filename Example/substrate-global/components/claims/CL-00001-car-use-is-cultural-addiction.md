# Car Use Is Cultural Addiction Not Necessity

## Summary

Car dependency is not a rational necessity but a cultural addiction built over decades through advertising, infrastructure, and social norms — analogous to smoking addiction — requiring treatment-style intervention rather than accommodation.

## Details

This claim asserts that car use in cities is an addiction comparable to tobacco dependency. It developed through decades of advertising, cultural references, assumed aspirations, and environment design. Like smokers who struggle to admit their addiction and excuse fellow smokers, car-dependent societies excuse dangerous driving and resist change. The parallel with smoking is instructive: 80% to 12% decline in UK smoking rates, public smoking now socially unacceptable. The same cultural shift is possible for car use through a combination of government action (road layout changes prioritising people) and individual behaviour change ("think before we drive").
