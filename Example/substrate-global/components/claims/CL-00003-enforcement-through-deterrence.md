# Enforcement Through Deterrence Is Counterproductive

## Summary

The claim that using enforcement and fortification as a deterrence strategy for migration is fundamentally counterproductive — it does not stop migration but instead makes the journey more lethal, forces migrants to rely on professional smugglers, and empowers criminal organizations like cartels to control smuggling routes and charge higher fees.

## Details

The US Border Patrol's "Prevention Through Deterrence" strategy, implemented in the 1990s, was based on the theory that fortifying urban crossing areas would deter migrants by funneling them into remote, harsh terrain where the environment itself would serve as a barrier. The evidence from the San Antonio trailer disaster and decades of border policy outcomes suggests the opposite occurred. Independent border crossing became nearly impossible, creating a captive market for professional smugglers. Smuggling organizations like Cártel del Noreste gained power, profitability, and control over migration routes. The policy incentivized increasingly dangerous smuggling methods — including sealed tractor-trailers — to bypass checkpoints. Rather than deterring migration, enforcement-through-deterrence made it more organized, more expensive, and more lethal. The San Antonio disaster, where 53 migrants died in a sealed trailer, is a direct consequence of this policy failure.
