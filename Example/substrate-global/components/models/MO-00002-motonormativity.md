# Motonormativity

## Summary

A psychological model describing how social norms cause people to excuse dangerous driving behaviour and apply double standards to cars versus other activities. Even non-drivers subconsciously excuse law-breaking and dangerous behaviour by drivers.

## Details

Coined by Walker, Davis, and Tapp (2023), motonormativity describes the ingrained assumption that "the driver is right and has all the rights." People excuse behaviour in cars they would not tolerate in other contexts — aggressive revving, hooting, speeding, law-breaking. Infrastructure changes that force driver attention (parklets, chicanes, optical illusions) are blamed for incidents caused by dangerous driving, not the drivers themselves. Lorries parked on roads take more space than parklets but are not criticised. The model explains why urban motorway designs persist despite evidence they harm communities.
