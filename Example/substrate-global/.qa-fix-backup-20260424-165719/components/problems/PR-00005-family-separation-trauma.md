# Family Separation and Trauma

## Summary

The San Antonio disaster and the broader smuggling trade cause devastating trauma to families of victims. Families lose loved ones, must travel internationally to identify remains, arrange repatriation, and cope with grief — often without adequate support.

## Details

The human cost of the smuggling trade extends far beyond the immediate victims. Families of the 53 deceased migrants in the San Antonio disaster endured multiple layers of trauma: the initial loss, the uncertainty of not knowing what happened, the journey to San Antonio to identify remains, the logistics of repatriation, and the long-term grief. Families like those of Begaí and Mariano Santiago Hipólito (where one brother survived and the other died), Delfina Bacajol, Virgilia López, Luz Estrella Cuevas Remolino, and María Antonia Torres Morales all experienced this devastating pattern. The trauma is compounded by the fact that many families live in poverty in their home countries and lack resources for international travel, legal assistance, and mental health support. The broader smuggling trade creates ongoing family separation as migrants leave home countries, often never to return.
